**Martín Virgili** (1976)

Holds a degree in Arts, music track (UBA). Composer, guitarist, teacher, and arts researcher. He studied composition with Ricardo Capellano and Gabriel Valverde, and musical aesthetics with Margarita Fernández. He has received grants from the Université de Paris 1 (Panthéon-Sorbonne), the University of Helsinki, the Fondo Nacional de las Artes, the Fundación Telefónica de Argentina, and the Centro Cultural España in Buenos Aires. His works have been presented in Argentina, Chile, Finland, Colombia, Venezuela, Canada, Mexico, Switzerland, and the United States, and premiered by renowned ensembles and soloists. He has taught courses and seminars at Duke University (North Carolina, USA); at the Universidad Javeriana, the Universidad CORPAS, and the Academia Superior de Artes in Bogotá, Colombia; at ESMUC (Escola Superior de Música de Catalunya) in Barcelona, Spain; at the CNA (Centro Nacional de las Artes) in Mexico City; and at CMMAS (Centro Mexicano para la Música y las Artes Sonoras) in Morelia, Michoacán. In Argentina, he served as a director and instructor at CEAMC (Centro de Estudios Avanzados en Música Contemporánea) and taught courses and seminars at the Universidad Tecnológica Nacional and the Universidad Nacional de Luján. He has collaborated with artists including Jacques Demierre (Switzerland); Vincent Barras (Switzerland); Margarita Fernández; Llorenç Barber (Spain); Guillermo Bocanegra (Colombia); Daniel Áñez (Colombia); Rodolfo Acosta (Colombia); Simon Steen-Andersen (Denmark); Ana María Romano (Colombia); Luca Belcastro (Italy); Elsa Justel; Pablo Jivotovschii; Mariano Malamud; Mariano Losi; Inés Sabatini; Guillermo Lema; among others. He directed the films "Desde las montañas" (2009, 52') and "Fluctuat Nec Mergitur" (2014), and has authored various publications on musicology and musical aesthetics. He currently forms, with Pamela Guruciaga, the duo Orange Music, performing pieces from the musical, theatrical, and music-theater repertoire. He is a member of the Club del Dibujo (Mar del Plata chapter) and directs the Orquesta Inestable del Club del Dibujo. He is a member of the Ensamble Orquesta Negra together with guitarist Guillermo Bocanegra and playwright Gastón Maziéres. He is a founding partner, together with designer Jorge L. Ihlenfeld, of the negra40.com project.

**Listening:** a material history of Western sound perception.

## Summary

To propose a history of listening is to attempt a history of relation. Listening, beyond aesthetics and acoustics, is an exercise in (re)knowing the world and the other. Surrounded by matter in motion, every thing has a note, a vibration that identifies and distinguishes it. The mesh that makes up physical existence is nothing but the sum of all those partial notes gathered into one vast, inscrutable sonic whole. Whoever listens, sees the world: sound registers its material and symbolic trace. Under these terms, this workshop will trace an aesthetic and social history of the sonic trajectory of the West, establishing, alongside it, a musical history of listening as appropriate.

Those of us who hear cannot help but listen. Wherever we go, a sonic frieze accompanies our actions and our intentions. Hearing is the first sense that connects us to the world, many months before all the others. Our ear, in turn, vividly describes for us the texture of things invisible and distant, and warns us of lurking dangers. Its hand touches what hides behind walls and behind the night. A lie can escape almost everything except the attentive perception of one who knows how to read the timbre of a cracking voice. Fear and understanding travel through the air: an unusual sound can keep us up all night. A sincere word can change our life.

In the same way, sounds establish the web that relates the subject to the socius, shaping a collective sonic network without which life would not be possible. The noises of labor, of agreements, of conspiracies. Wherever a social form exists, there is sound. Silence, for that very reason, marks the alliance between solitude and muteness: "Among birds, the vulture is the only one that sacrificed the flock for solitude, and song for elevation" (Quignard, 1998). Societies thus take part in an uninterrupted, atavistic sonic design, and the manner and determinations of those sounds trace the subtle outline of their politics, economies, mentalities, and aesthetics.

Following Attali, we can see that Listening may be thought through three sonic instances: (a) music used in rituals to make people forget generalized violence; (b) the use of music to instill belief in the harmony of the world, in order and in economic exchange; and finally (c) music that serves to silence, through the incessant reproduction of deafening sounds that drown out the remaining noises made by human beings. Against any of these forms of power, listening marks its own limit and its antidote. Whoever listens goes out, walks, moves.
